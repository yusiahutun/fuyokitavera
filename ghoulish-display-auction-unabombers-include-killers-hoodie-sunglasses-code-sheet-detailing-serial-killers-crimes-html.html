Is this really the best way to help his victims? 'Ghoulish' collection of Unabomber's personal possessions to go on sale

A ghoulish collection of clothes, sunglasses and handwritten notes belonging to the notorious unabomber murderer have been revealed as further items to be auctioned off by the U.S. Government.

Ted Kaczynski, who conducted a two decade-long mail bombing spree which killed three and injured 23 others, will have his personal effects auctioned off to raise money for his victims.

It was recently revealed that with millions of dollars still owed to his victims in compensation, U.S. marshals are to conduct an online sale of items including his driving licenses, birth certificates, and other effects.

Killer's clothing: The top and sunglasses belonging to Unabomber Ted Kaczynski are displayed on a table as part of an auction of the killer's possessions

Sale: Personal effects including Kaczynski's typewriters, photographs and books are also up for sale

New pictures of the unabomber's possessions set to be put up for sale include a dark green hoodie and sunglasses, as well as a handwritten manifesto and even a diploma certificate from Harvard.

Kaczynski's watches, typewriters, several hundred books and over 20,000 pages of handwritten documents will also be sold.

The pages include the original handwritten and typewritten versions of his 'Industrial Society and its Future,' also known as the 'Unabom Manifesto'.

The auction started yesterday and will continue until June 2.

Prized possessions: Six pairs of sunglasses form another part of the auction

Detailed: Handwritten manuscripts and pages are among the thousands of documents included in the auction

Insightful: Photographs and books belonging to the Unabomber have been included in the lot which runs until June 2

Certificate: A diploma from Harvard is among the stranger items being sold in the auction

Decoded: The sheet of numbers written by Kaczynski, which was deciphered by the FBI, included discussions of his crimes

Kaczynski was dubbed the 'unabomber' by the FBI as shorthand for his early targets of universities and airlines.

The bombings were said to be part of his supposed world vision that human freedom was being eroded by modern technology, outlined in his papers.

Notorious: Ted J. Kaczynski in his booking mug shot in April 1996

The irony of selling his belongings online, considering the killer's views on technology, was deliberate, according to U.S. Marshals.

'We will use the technology that Kaczynski railed against in his various manifestos to sell artefacts of his life,' Marshal Albert NÃ¡jera said.

'The proceeds will go to his victims and, in a very small way, offset some of the hardships they have suffered.'

The sale was approved last August as an attempt to apply the proceeds to the $15 million in restitution Kaczynski has been ordered to pay four of his victims.

Kaczynski was jailed in 1998 and has been fighting a long-running legal battle from behind bars over the sale of his belongings.

The killer had filed motions in both the 9th Circuit and a lower court objecting to the auction.

'I have not filed a petition to the Supreme Court ... because I have not been able to find representation for such an effort,' the killer wrote on one motion filed in June 2009.

'My experiences in the federal courts to date have convinced me that any pro se effort on my part is futile, no matter how strong its legal basis may be, and that I can hope for success only with the help of a lawyer who has the skill to influence judges on other than legal grounds.'

Compensation: Kaczynski was ordered to pay around $15million in restitution to four of his victims but was fighting plans to auction his belongings to fund the payment

Kaczynski, who was born in Chicago, was arrested in April 1996 after a mail bombing spree that spanned nearly 20 years from 1978 to 1995.

The infamous killer, now 68, was seized by authorities at the remote cabin outside Lincoln, Montana, where he lived, after a tip from his brother.

The cabin, which had no running water or indoor plumbing, has since been rebuilt and is now exhibited at the Newseum in Washington.

Reign of terror: A quiver of arrows was also among his possessions

Rambling thoughts of a killer: Thousands of pages of handwritten and typed manuscripts are in the sale

Kaczynski pleaded guilty in January 1998 and was sentenced to life in prison without parole. He is now serving in the federal 'Supermax' prison in Florence, Colorado.

'I regard him as the essence of evil,' Dr. Charles Epstein, one of the four victims, said in 2009. 'He has no compassion.'

Epstein, who has since passed away, was seriously injured in 1993 when he opened his mail and a bomb went off.

He lost parts of three fingers in the blast, which also destroyed his eardrums.
