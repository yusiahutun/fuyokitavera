Mtumba Hawker Becomes Tycoon, Builds multi-Million Business After Several Failures: &quot;Mungu Tu&quot;

  • Those who saw Alex Malova Kidake grow up in a poverty-stricken polygamous family may have written him off
  • The 20th-born is the only educated one among 19 illiterate siblings, thanks to his desire to change his story
  • He ventured into the mtumba business and built his wealth meticulously to the point of owning a multi-million business empire
  • He now owns over 10 cars, a house in Dubai, another in Lavington, and a third in Nangili, Sabatia county

Alex Malova Kidake is a fine gentleman, business magnate, and tycoon with palatial houses in Dubai, Lavington, and Kakamega County.

He dons clean-cut suits, polished outfits, and designer shoes that accentuate the proper image of a man with deep pockets.

But there is a story that very few people know: he was born in abject poverty.

Started as mtumba hawker

Born in a polygamous family that wallowed in squalor, the 20th born from the second wife grew up struggling with basic needs.

In an exclusive interview with TUKO.co.ke, Kidake disclosed that out of the 20 siblings, he is the only one educated; the rest dropped out of school due to lack of fees.

After excelling in high school, the young, ambitious man could not go to university because his parents couldn't afford it.

"I moved to Nairobi to live with my brother and started hawking clothes. It is here that I met Caroline Muthoni, another budding hawker," he started.

Ran into failure

The two became business partners, started living together, and were soon proud parents to a bouncing little human.

Over time, the couple transitioned into the computer business and eventually established a thriving shop on Biashara Street.

From trekking door to door hawking, Kidake now owned a Toyota RAV4, a prestigious vehicle by the standards back then.

"The newfound wealth changed who I was because I started hanging out with other rich fellas and forgot about the business. Before I knew it, the business had collapsed and we went back to zero," he recalled.

Failed second time

For a man who had built from scratch, he went back to the drawing board and rebuilt the business afresh, and before long, he was back at the top.

Kidake relocated from the modest KSh 2,500 house in Githurai and moved his family to Dubai, where his wife and three children now reside.

Due to his love for knowledge, he returned to school and picked up the journey from where he left earlier.

"Failure came calling once again and financial troubles led to the auction of 14 business cars. Apart from that, several of my containers were destroyed at the port in Mombasa," he continued.

Kidake Foundation

Once again, Kidake was back on his knees and bounced back through Scratch Homes and Kidake Intercontinental, a marketing company in Dubai.

To give back to the community he started the Kidake Foundation to give back to the community that had supported him in his journey.

"One of the biggest projects in the works is a mass wedding on December 12 at Ivona Grounds in Chavakali, where I want to support young people to settle into marriage," he revealed.

He says this has never happened in his home county but will go a long way in helping young couples solemnise their unions.

Kidake's Residence

When he is not in the public doing business or serving his people, he retreats to the Kidake Residence, a palatial home in Nangili, Kakamega, with 14 bedrooms and a giant swimming pool.

He says he went all out with the house because he had come from a family where land and housing were a problem.

His compound also boasts 18 Toyota Land Cruisers, several servants' quarters, and top-notch security systems.

From a family where people didn't go to school, Kidake is currently pursuing a Master's in Finance.

"Poverty is bad. It strips you of every shred of dignity," he muttered.

Third time failing

When the COVID-19 pandemic hit, Kidake had to close shop, prompting him to venture into politics.

"I wasnâ€™t interested in politics because I was green and had several bad experiences with them, but I listened to advice from all over and plunged in," he recalled.

Like the two previous experiences, Kidake was at the bottom again after spending all his money on campaigns.

He admits to making grave mistakes but does not regret them, as he picked valuable lessons to guide him as he has his eyes set on becoming the Vihiga governor in the next election.

"Many people don't know this, but I became so broke on voting day that I had to take a matatu to go to the polling station," said Kidake.
