Chlo Grace Moretz proposes a solution to Hollywood's diversity problem

As a former child star, Chloë Grace Moretz is well acquainted with Hollywood misogyny. At 15, she was fat-shamed by a male co-star; a year later she was encouraged to wear a push-up bra. Moretz’s new movie – the Sundance prize-winner The Miseducation of Cameron Post – is masterminded by the director Desiree Akhavan. Being a queer woman of colour, the film-maker belongs to a demographic that is all but invisible in cinema.

Since representation so often starts at the top, the actress was eager to sign onto a project with Akhavan at the helm. “It’s a trickle-down effect,” Moretz tells Bazaar. “You have to hire more women in positions of power.” Akhavan agrees that there is no magical “diversity formula” that will solve the business’ gender problem. “People don’t like women,” she says. "We live in an unfair world where women’s rights are still at the bottom of the pile. I really know and experience that the longer I work in this industry.”

The Miseducation of Cameron Post is a queer coming-of-age story that quietly unravels identity politics. When she is caught fooling around with her female friend on prom night, Cameron (Moretz) is shipped off to God’s Promise, a Christian correctional facility that promises to ‘cure’ her homosexuality. Despite being set in the early Nineties, Cameron Post is eerily of-the-moment: nearly 700,000 Americans have undergone conversion therapy and, although the practice has been discredited by psychiatrists, vice-president Mike Pence has spoken out in its favour.

Moretz, who has long been an advocate of LGBTQ+ rights, jumped at the chance to headline a film with such an important message. “It gave me the opportunity to meld my love of activism with my art form,” she says.
